Mentor Guide

Effective Grading

  • Begin feedback with what the student did well to build confidence.
  • Use simple, clear rubrics focused on 2–3 key skills, especially for the Constructed Response.
  • Give credit for correct setup and reasoning, even when arithmetic slips lead to the wrong final number.
  • Provide specific, actionable feedback (e.g., "check your sign when dividing by a negative" rather than just "wrong").
  • Allow flexible response formats (talking through steps aloud, using a calculator, or drawing diagrams) when writing is difficult.
  • Focus on the student's growth by comparing their work to their own previous attempts.

Teaching Concepts Students May Have Missed

  • Break multi-step word problems into smaller steps: identify what's given, what's asked, then choose an operation.
  • Use visual supports for geometry (draw the triangle, label the legs and hypotenuse) and for data questions (highlight the relevant table cells or graph points).
  • Model one problem fully, then guide the student through a similar one, then have them try independently.
  • Connect probability questions to familiar real-life examples (raffles, dice, decks of cards) before introducing the without-replacement wrinkle.
  • Revisit sign rules for inequalities (flipping the symbol when dividing by a negative) since this is a common point of confusion.
  • Review key formulas frequently (area, perimeter, volume, slope) and check for understanding before moving on.
  • Reduce anxiety by allowing breaks and normalizing that multi-step problems take longer to work through.

General Tips for Mentors

  • Be patient and encouraging — celebrate small successes, especially with multi-step problems.
  • Adapt materials when needed (larger print, graph paper, a four-function calculator) since calculators are permitted on the real GED Math test.
  • Build student confidence and self-advocacy around asking for clarification on word problems.
  • Make problems relevant to the student's goals and daily life (budgeting, shopping discounts, measuring spaces).
  • Remind students that the real GED Mathematical Reasoning test is 115 minutes long, and help them build pacing habits during practice.

ANSWER KEY & EXPLANATIONS

For Instructor / Self-Check Use Only • Student version should not include this page

1. A — Total coffee sales: 312 + 378 + 295 + 425 + 510 + 620 + 480 = $3,020.
2. B — Tuesday has the highest combined revenue per customer: (378+112) ÷ 69 ≈ $7.10, the highest of all seven days.
3. C — Food total $963; grand total revenue $3,983. 963 ÷ 3,983 ≈ 24.2%, which rounds to 24%.
4. D — Highest coffee sales $620 (Saturday), lowest $295 (Wednesday). Range = 620 − 295 = $325.
5. A — Week-to-week changes are +250, −70, +220, +150. The largest increase is +250, from Week 1 to Week 2.
6. B — Sum of revenues (1,200 + 1,450 + 1,380 + 1,600 + 1,750) = 7,380 ÷ 5 = $1,476.
7. C — Increase from Week 4 to Week 5 is +150 (1,600 → 1,750). Projecting the same increase gives 1,750 + 150 = $1,900.
8. B — Revenue rises overall from $1,200 to $1,750, with exactly one weekly decrease (Week 2 to Week 3).
9. A — Pythagorean theorem: 6² + 8² = 36 + 64 = 100. √100 = 10 ft.
10. C — Perimeter = 6 + 8 + 10 = 24 feet.
11. A — Area of right triangle = (6 × 8) ÷ 2 = 24 square feet.
12. D — Doubling the legs gives 12 ft and 16 ft, with hypotenuse 20 ft (a scaled 3-4-5 triangle). New perimeter = 12 + 16 + 20 = 48 ft.
13. A — Probability = Jamal's tickets ÷ total tickets = 12/300 = 1/25.
14. B — P(not winning grand prize) = 1 − 12/300 = 288/300 = 24/25.
15. A — After the grand-prize ticket is removed (not Jamal's), 299 tickets remain and Jamal still holds 12 of them: 12/299.
16. B — Every individual ticket has the same 1/300 chance of being drawn for the grand prize, regardless of who holds it.
17. A — 4x − 9 = 19 → 4x = 28 → x = 7.
18. A — Distribute: 3(x+4) − 2x = 3x + 12 − 2x = x + 12.
19. A — Area = length × width = 15 × 8 = 120 square inches.
20. B — Slope m = (13 − 5) ÷ (6 − 2) = 8 ÷ 4 = 2.
21. A — 25% of 48 = 12. Sale price = 48 − 12 = $36.
22. A — −3x + 7 > 16 → −3x > 9 → x < −3 (the inequality flips when dividing by a negative number).
23. C — Volume = πr²h ≈ 3.14 × 3² × 10 = 3.14 × 9 × 10 = 282.6 cubic inches.
24. A — f(4) = 2(4)² − 5 = 2(16) − 5 = 32 − 5 = 27.

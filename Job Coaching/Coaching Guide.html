Core Philosophy of Job Coaching

Job coaching for interns with intellectual and developmental disabilities is built on dignity, self-determination, and fading support. The goal is never “mentor-dependent success” but success that lasts after the mentor steps back. Every strategy below is designed to honor the intern’s strengths while systematically building independence through natural supports.

1. Task Analysis — How to Break Down Any Job Duty

Task analysis is the foundation of effective coaching. Break every duty into the smallest possible observable steps so the intern can succeed one action at a time.

Steps to Create a Strong Task Analysis
  1. Watch a skilled coworker perform the full task from start to finish.
  2. Write one clear, observable action per step (avoid vague words like “get ready” or “clean up”).
  3. Identify the natural cue in the environment that tells the intern when to start the step.
  4. Note any accommodations the intern may need (visuals, positioning, tool modification).
  5. Test the steps with the intern and adjust based on what actually works.
Example 1: Greeting a Customer / Visitor
1. Stand in visible spot with friendly posture
2. Make eye contact and smile when customer approaches
3. Say: “Hello! Welcome to [Worksite]. How can I help you today?”
4. Listen to customer’s request
5. Provide clear information or direction
6. End with: “Is there anything else I can help with?” + smile
Example 2: Taking & Organizing Photos (Documentation Role)
1. Check daily photo assignment list with supervisor
2. Go to assigned area and look for good lighting/angles
3. Take 3–5 photos of the subject
4. Review photos on camera screen (delete blurry ones)
5. Show best 1–2 photos to supervisor for feedback
6. Save photos in correct folder with clear file name

2. Least-to-Most Prompting Hierarchy

Always start with the least intrusive prompt that allows success. This respects the intern’s ability and tells you exactly what level of support is truly needed.

I
Independent
V
Verbal
M
Model / Show
G
Gestural
P
Physical
Key Rules:
• Give 3–5 seconds of wait time before moving to the next prompt level.
• Record only the lowest prompt level that produced correct performance.
• Physical prompts should be used only when necessary and faded immediately.
• Always pair prompts with natural cues so the intern learns to respond to the environment, not just you.

3. Fading Prompts & Building Natural Supports

The ultimate goal is for the intern to succeed using supports that exist naturally in the workplace (coworkers, visuals, routines, and self-management).

When to Start Fading
  • Intern performs step correctly 80–85%+ across multiple sessions
  • Intern begins responding to natural workplace cues
  • Intern self-corrects or asks for help appropriately
Four Types of Natural Supports
  • Coworker / Peer: Natural modeling and quick help
  • Visual / Environmental: Checklists, labels, picture sequences
  • Supervisor Routines: Regular brief positive feedback
  • Self-Management: Intern uses own tools or knows when to ask

4. Creating Physical Tools & Learning Materials

Many interns benefit greatly from tangible, portable supports. These tools can be created quickly using the task analysis you already built in the Worksheet tab.

Laminated Keychain Cards (Highly Recommended)
  • Print 4–6 key steps on a small card (business card size or 3x5)
  • Laminate and punch a hole in the corner
  • Attach to a keychain or lanyard the intern can carry
  • Great for greeting scripts, safety steps, or photo naming rules
  • Update easily by printing new versions
Picture Sequence Strips & Checklists
  • Use simple photos or icons for each step
  • Print, laminate, and connect with a ring or velcro
  • Intern can flip through the strip as they work
  • Paper checklists can be placed at the workstation
  • Color-code steps (green = start, yellow = next, red = finish)
Quick Tip: Use the task breakdown you created in Section 2 of the Worksheet. Copy the observable steps into a Word or Google Doc, add simple images or icons (use free sites like Pixabay or take your own photos), print, laminate, and cut. These physical tools dramatically increase independence and reduce prompt dependency.

5. Turning Task Analysis into Custom Learning Materials

The task breakdowns you create in this worksheet are the perfect starting point for creating personalized learning materials.

Step-by-step process:
  1. Complete a detailed task breakdown in the Worksheet tab.
  2. Copy the steps into a simple document or slide.
  3. Add one photo or simple icon next to each step (use phone camera or free image sites).
  4. Print on cardstock or regular paper.
  5. Laminate for durability (office supply store or home laminator).
  6. Create multiple versions: full strip, keychain size, and workstation poster.
  7. Review with the intern and let them help choose colors or photos when possible.
Pro Tip: Involve the intern in making the materials when possible. This increases ownership and helps them understand the steps even before they practice the task.

6. Additional Job Coaching Principles

• Start with Strengths

Begin every session by naming something the intern already does well or has improved. This builds confidence and motivation.

• Use Specific Praise

Instead of “Good job,” say “You remembered the greeting script on your own — that was excellent thinking.”

• Connect to Interests

Link tasks to things the intern enjoys or is good at (e.g., “Your eye for detail from photography will help these displays look great”).

• Data is Your Friend

Use the Session Log to track independence percentage and prompt levels. This shows real progress and guides when to fade support.

• Collaborate with the Team

Share successful strategies with supervisors and coworkers so everyone uses the same consistent approach.

• Celebrate Natural Supports

When a coworker helps or the intern uses their own checklist successfully, celebrate it loudly — that is the real win.
